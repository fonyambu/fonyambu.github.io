We have built an impressive literature on why African genomes matter. We have not built a comparable pipeline for generating them. It is time to change the ratio.

There is no shortage of writing on the underrepresentation of African populations in global genomic datasets. In the past eighteen months, journals across the Nature family have published a notable run of work, much of it by African-led teams, articulating this disparity with growing precision. In June 2026, a Comment in *Nature Communications* led by [Mohamed Zahir Alimohamed and senior-authored by Michele Ramsay](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-74952-7) made the case for national genome projects in Africa, noting that only 0.15 percent of participants in genome-wide association studies are drawn from African populations. Earlier in the year, the [AGenDA consortium published its governance and community-engagement framework](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-025-09935-7) in *Nature*. A separate *Nature Communications* paper examined benefit-sharing models in South African genomics. Reviews, perspectives, and readiness assessments have appeared steadily in *Human Molecular Genetics*, *npj Genomic Medicine*, and elsewhere.

These are substantive contributions. The scholarship on ethical frameworks, governance, consent, and benefit-sharing has matured considerably, and it would be a mistake to wave it away. African-led governance of African data is a genuine achievement, hard-won against a long history of extraction.

But a pattern is becoming difficult to ignore. The literature on why African genomes should be generated is expanding faster than the number of African genomes actually being generated. The field's most visible output has become the argument for data rather than the data itself. A perspective piece can move from concept to acceptance in months and requires no sequencing budget, no multi-jurisdictional ethics approval, no cold chain, and no compute. A population-scale sequencing programme requires all of these and takes years. The asymmetry in effort is exactly mirrored by the asymmetry in what gets published.

This matters for a practical reason. Each new commentary that restates the 0.15 percent figure without adding sequence data documents the gap more precisely while leaving it the same size.

## The projects that are actually sequencing

It is worth being specific about the initiatives that have moved past advocacy into production, because they are the models worth emulating.

Senegal offers two complementary examples. The [SEN-GENOME project](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajhg.2024.06.012), described by Gaye and colleagues, takes a decentralized national approach, with the aim of sequencing 1,000 genomes from 31 ethnolinguistic groups across the country's fourteen administrative regions. Separately, in June 2026, the [Institut Pasteur de Dakar](https://www.pasteur.sn/) reported sequencing the first 40 human whole genomes in Senegal using two platforms in parallel, Illumina NovaSeq and Oxford Nanopore, and coupled this directly to a clinical application: early detection of breast cancer. The IPD effort is small in number but instructive in design. It links sequencing infrastructure to a defined clinical endpoint and to patients, rather than stopping at a position statement.

In Ghana, [Yemaachi Biotech](https://www.yemaachi.com/), with Roche as anchor partner, has launched The African Cancer Atlas (TACA), which aims to generate genomic and clinical data from up to 7,500 African cancer patients, producing up to 15,000 tumour and paired-normal genomes. Yemaachi has built whole-genome sequencing capacity domestically, a first for the country, and runs the project through a consortium of clinical sites across multiple African countries. TACA is an example of an African-led entity generating clinically annotated data at scale. It is also, candidly, dependent on pharmaceutical backing through Roche and Illumina, which is precisely what makes its economics sustainable rather than tied to a single grant cycle. That dependency is a feature of the model, not a flaw to be hidden, provided the governance terms keep African investigators in control of the data.

The [AGenDA project](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-025-09935-7), under the [H3Africa](https://h3africa.org/) umbrella, has generated whole-genome data from more than 1,000 individuals across nine countries, deliberately sampling hunter-gatherer communities, Nilo-Saharan and Afro-Asiatic speakers, and understudied Bantu groups. This is real work, and the consortium's transparency about its process is valuable in its own right. At the [Centre for Molecular Biosystems](https://www.cmbgenomics.org), the Kenya Reference Genome Initiative is sequencing high-depth whole genomes across Kenyan ethnolinguistic groups, with an emphasis on pharmacogenomics, HLA typing, and structural variation, the features of the genome that short, shallow, Eurocentric references serve worst.

What these projects share is a willingness to do the unglamorous work: sample collection, ethics approval across borders, logistics, sequencing runs, and variant calling. None of it produces the clean, quotable output of a well-turned perspective. All of it produces genomes.

## The access asymmetry that is rarely stated plainly

There is a less comfortable dimension to this conversation, and it concerns the data that already exists.

A meaningful share of African genomic data sits behind data access committees (DACs) and embargo windows that, in practice, are difficult for many African scientists to navigate. As [Nchangwi Syntia Munung](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40142-024-00211-0) of the University of Cape Town has noted, the H3Africa data-sharing policy includes an eleven-month publication embargo allowing consortium investigators to analyse and share data internally before it is released to the broader community under controlled access. The stated and entirely legitimate rationale is to protect African investigators, working with limited resources, from being outpaced by better-funded groups elsewhere.

The intention is sound. The downstream effect is more ambiguous. Munung observes that such embargoes and restrictive protocols, though designed to protect African researchers, may inadvertently encourage data hoarding, and that researchers who publicly commit to data sharing often fail to provide access when asked. The cohort-profile literature documents a related failure mode: for some H3Africa projects, investigators received their own data so late in the funding cycle that they lacked the resources to complete quality control, deposition, analysis, and publication. Protection, in other words, can shade into obstruction even when no one intends it.

The result is a structural irony. African genomes, collected from African communities under African ethical oversight, are deposited in the [European Genome-phenome Archive](https://ega-archive.org/) and released through formal DAC applications. AGenDA's own framework involves two access committees, one internal to the consortium and one for external requests, with industry access permitted but subject to additional layers of review. This is responsible governance on its face. The open question is whether the cumulative effect serves the broad community of African clinicians, pharmacogeneticists, and translational researchers, or whether it concentrates practical access among a small number of well-established groups while everyone else waits in a queue.

I want to be precise here, because this argument is easy to misread. Participant protection is non-negotiable. Data sovereignty is non-negotiable. The critique is not that governance exists; it is that the design of a governance regime has consequences, and a regime whose practical effect is that non-consortium African scientists cannot readily use African data is not fully serving its own stated purpose. This is not an outsider's complaint. It is a point made by African scholars working inside the very consortia that built these systems, which is the strongest possible signal that it deserves a serious answer rather than a defensive one.

There is also a governance-design question worth naming without personalizing it. When the same small community writes the influential perspectives, leads the flagship consortia, and sits on the committees that adjudicate access, a bottleneck can form that is institutional rather than scientific. The remedy is not to question anyone's motives. It is to separate these functions by design, so that access decisions do not depend on proximity to the people who generated or champion the data.

## What progress would actually look like

Four things, concretely.

**Generate more data, under your own governance.** The most reliable way past an access bottleneck is to produce new data rather than queue for old data controlled elsewhere. SEN-GENOME, TACA, IPD Dakar, and KRGI are all variations on this principle.

**Treat sequencing as something Africa does, not something done to African samples elsewhere.** The economics have shifted. Long-read platforms from Oxford Nanopore and high-throughput systems from Ultima Genomics and Illumina have brought deep whole-genome sequencing within reach of a well-organized laboratory with modest capital. The notion that genome generation can only happen at a handful of legacy centres is no longer technically true. Where it persists, it persists as an institutional habit, not a hardware constraint.

**Rebalance the funding portfolio.** For every grant supporting a review, framework, or commentary, there should be several supporting wet-lab work, sample collection, and sequencing. The intellectual case has been made, and made well. It does not need to be made again, in the same journals, by the same authors.

**Engage the access question honestly.** Federated analysis, data-visiting models, and stewardship arrangements that keep control local while permitting analysis are not abstractions; they are the practical alternative to embargo-and-DAC as the default. Munung and others have already sketched the direction. What is missing is the will to treat ease of African access as a primary design goal rather than an afterthought.

## The call is simple

Generate genomes. Build the infrastructure to generate them on the continent. Make the resulting data genuinely accessible to African scientists, without requiring them to navigate processes that were designed, in some cases, more to manage credit than to protect participants. Support the translational and commercial entities turning sequence into clinical and economic value, because that is what makes the whole enterprise sustainable rather than perpetually grant-dependent.

The next paper on African genomics that I would most like to read has a methods section: a platform, a coverage depth, a sample size, and a data-access model that an unaffiliated researcher in Nairobi or Kumasi or Dakar could actually use. Not another account of why this matters.

We already know why it matters. The task now is to do it.
